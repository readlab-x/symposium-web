# Project Guidance

## Design Context

### Users
Primary users are university classrooms and researchers reading and studying Plato's *Symposium*. They use the product for sustained reading, annotation, entity tracking, thematic comparison, and relation browsing. The interface should support long sessions, quick scholarly scanning, and low-friction cross-reference between text and interpretation.

### Brand Personality
Restrained, academic, curated.

The interface should evoke calm concentration, editorial trust, and scholarly seriousness. It should feel like a reading desk or exhibition apparatus rather than a product demo.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use an editorial, research-oriented visual language inspired by annotated editions, exhibition labels, and library reading rooms. Preserve the warm paper-like neutrals and culturally grounded tone already present in the codebase. Support both light and dark mode.

Motion may be noticeable and ceremonial, but it must always remain smooth, purposeful, and compatible with `prefers-reduced-motion`. There is no strong external visual reference to imitate; the product should derive its identity from internal consistency rather than trend-following.

Explicit anti-directions:
- Do not use tech-glow aesthetics.
- Do not make the site feel like a SaaS dashboard.

### Design Principles
1. Text comes first. Reading clarity always outranks decorative UI.
2. Favor restraint over spectacle. Atmosphere should support the content, not compete with it.
3. Treat tools, filters, and panels as scholarly apparatus, not product chrome.
4. Keep interaction states explicit, but keep resting states quiet.
5. Use motion to guide attention and interpretation, never to delay or distract.
6. Preserve cultural warmth and editorial gravity; avoid generic startup or futuristic styling.
